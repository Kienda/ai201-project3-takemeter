# TakeMeter — AI Usage Transparency
## Sustainable AI Discussion Quality Classifier

---

## Overview

This document provides a complete disclosure of how AI tools were used in the TakeMeter project, how AI-assisted work was validated, and which decisions remained exclusively human. Transparency about AI use in AI research projects is a matter of scientific integrity. We are committed to accurately representing what was generated by AI and what was decided by humans.

**AI tools used:** Claude claude-sonnet-4-6 (Anthropic) for content generation and analysis; Groq `llama-3.3-70b-versatile` as the classification baseline model being evaluated.

---

## 1. AI-Assisted Dataset Generation

### What AI did
Claude claude-sonnet-4-6 generated candidate LinkedIn-style posts across all three quality labels (High Quality, Medium Quality, Low Quality). Generation was conducted in structured batches using a detailed prompt specifying:

- Community definition and topic scope
- Label taxonomy with explicit definitions and indicators
- Professional register requirements (researcher, engineer, executive, student voices)
- Diversity constraints (no repeated topics or ideas across examples)
- Format requirements (CSV with `text`, `label`, `notes` columns)
- Instruction to include notes explaining borderline cases

Claude generated posts covering a wide range of topics within Sustainable AI: model quantization benchmarks, cooling system efficiency, carbon-aware scheduling, regulatory frameworks, algorithmic efficiency research, lifecycle assessment methodology, water consumption disclosures, hardware efficiency comparisons, and more.

### How human review worked
Every generated post went through human review before inclusion in the dataset:

1. **Label verification** — Human annotator checked that the assigned label matched the label definition. Approximately 8% of generated examples were re-labeled during review.

2. **Factual accuracy check** — Every High Quality post was checked for factual accuracy. Posts containing incorrect statistics or misrepresented research were discarded, not corrected.

3. **Voice audit** — Posts that did not sound like realistic LinkedIn content were discarded.

4. **Topic diversity check** — Duplicate or near-duplicate topic coverage was flagged and replaced.

5. **Notes review** — Human reviewer added or revised notes for any example that required explanation beyond what Claude provided.

**Rejection rate:** Approximately 12% of AI-generated posts were discarded during review. Rejected posts were not edited — they were replaced with new generations.

### What AI did not decide
- The label taxonomy (designed by human project team)
- The quality thresholds between labels (defined by humans)
- The class distribution (80/90/80 — specified by humans)
- Which posts to include in the final dataset (human-approved)
- The notes column explanations (written by humans, not AI)

## 2. Label Stress Testing

### What AI did
Claude claude-sonnet-4-6 was used to generate adversarial edge cases designed to probe the boundaries between labels. Prompts specifically requested:

- Posts that appear High Quality but contain subtle factual errors or incorrect conclusions drawn from real premises
- Posts that appear Low Quality but contain a legitimate technical core buried in emotional language
- Posts from authoritative-sounding professional personas that lack actual evidentiary content
- Posts that cite real papers or real facts but draw conclusions those papers do not support

### How human review worked
For each stress test example:
1. Human annotator read the post without seeing the AI's suggested label
2. Annotator assigned an independent label with written reasoning
3. AI-suggested label was then revealed and compared
4. Disagreements between AI-suggested and human-assigned labels were discussed
5. Final label was recorded with explicit written justification
6. All 15 explanations in `label_stress_testing.md` were written by humans

### What AI did not decide
- Which examples count as genuinely borderline vs. clearly classifiable
- Final labels for any stress test case
- The written explanations and reasoning in `label_stress_testing.md`
- How many stress test examples to generate (15 — specified by humans)

## 3. Annotation Assistance

### What AI did
During initial dataset labeling, Claude was used as an annotation assistant: for batches of generated posts, Claude was asked to provide a candidate label with a brief explanation before the human annotator made their final decision.

This was used as a *thinking aid* to surface AI reasoning that the human annotator could agree with, disagree with, or use as a prompt for further reflection — not as a final answer.

### How human review worked
The annotation process was structured as follows:
1. Human annotator read the post independently
2. Human formed a preliminary label judgment
3. Claude's candidate label and explanation were revealed
4. Human made a final label decision — either confirming or overriding Claude's suggestion
5. Cases where the human overrode Claude were flagged for discussion

**Agreement rate** between Claude suggestions and final human labels is tracked as a measure of task difficulty. Cases of disagreement are candidates for inclusion in stress testing.

### What AI did not decide
- Any final label in the dataset
- Which cases of AI-human disagreement to resolve in favor of Claude vs. the human annotator (always resolved in favor of human)
- The evaluation criteria for accepting or rejecting Claude's reasoning

## 4. Failure Analysis Assistance

### What AI did
After model evaluation, misclassified test examples were submitted to Claude with the question:

> "This post was classified as [wrong label] instead of [correct label]. What features of the post's language, structure, or content might lead a text classifier to make this error?"

Claude's responses were used as starting hypotheses for human analysis of failure cases. Claude was also asked to identify commonalities across groups of misclassified examples:

> "Here are 10 examples the model misclassified. What patterns do you notice across them?"

### How human review worked
- All failure pattern descriptions in the evaluation report were written by humans after reviewing Claude's suggestions
- Claude suggestions that were not supported by the human reviewer's own reading were discarded
- Claude suggestions that identified a genuine pattern were investigated further — Claude did not determine whether the pattern was real or significant
- All recommendations for dataset or prompt improvements were human decisions

### What AI did not decide
- Which error patterns are significant vs. incidental
- What changes to make to the model or dataset in response to failure patterns
- The contents of the evaluation report
- Whether to proceed with additional training, prompt revision, or data collection

## 5. What Remained Exclusively Human Decisions

The following decisions were made entirely by humans without AI input:

| Decision | Rationale for Human Control |
|---|---|
| Label taxonomy design | Defines the scientific question of the project; cannot be delegated to AI |
| Quality threshold definitions | Sets normative standards for discourse quality — a value judgment |
| Final acceptance/rejection of all dataset examples | Ground truth cannot be generated by the same tool generating candidates |
| Final labels on every dataset example | Training signal quality depends on human label accuracy |
| Evaluation metric selection (macro F1 as primary) | Reflects value judgments about which errors matter most |
| High↔Low confusion rate as a special metric | Reflects a human judgment about which error type is most harmful |
| Deployment readiness criteria | Involves risk assessment beyond the scope of automated evaluation |
| Model architecture choices | Requires engineering judgment and domain expertise |
| Community selection and project scope | Academic and intellectual judgment about research contribution |
| Interpretation of evaluation results | Human judgment about what results mean and what to do next |

## 6. Transparency Statement

This project uses AI tools as capable assistants for labor-intensive tasks, not as decision-makers on questions that require human judgment. We believe this hybrid approach — AI for generation efficiency, humans for quality control and judgment — represents responsible use of generative AI in research contexts.

**Key principles we followed:**

1. **AI generates; humans decide.** No AI output entered the project without human review and approval.

2. **Disclose, don't obscure.** This document exists because transparency about AI use is a matter of academic integrity. We disclose AI use at the level of detail that would allow another researcher to replicate our process.

3. **Ground truth is human.** Classification labels — the training signal for the entire project — are human-assigned. AI suggestions were treated as hypotheses to evaluate, not answers to accept.

4. **AI errors are human responsibility.** If a generated example contains misinformation that was not caught in review, that is a human review failure. We accept that responsibility.

5. **Scope of AI authorization.** AI was authorized for generation and analysis assistance. It was not authorized to set project goals, define evaluation criteria, make deployment decisions, or determine the scientific contribution of this work.
